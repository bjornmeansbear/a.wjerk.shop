Spontaneous Lamps

Each act of making is an act of unmaking. Each design “solving” a problem creates a problem. To counteract this, Spontaneous Lamps are created from found, common materials — like a 5 gallon bucket, an extension cord, a clip on light, an extra lampshade, loose pebbles, rubble, and broken bricks, foraged bamboo. Spontaneous Lamps suppose that while one might need a lamp right now, one might not need that lamp tomorrow: so, how can the materials used in constructing the lamp return to their original states and continue being raw materials awaiting a next use.

Spontaneous Lamps is an ongoing, ad hoc project: something in the house needs a lamp, so whatever’s around gets gathered into one. Five-gallon buckets, bricks and rocks pulled from a rubble heap in an alley, bamboo cut down and cleared from the yard, the guts of a clip-on painter’s light pulled apart and rewired into something else — once, even the lamp’s own cord doubling as part of a tensegrity structure, just to hold a reading light over someone’s shoulder in a rocking chair, for a few hours.

Built to come back apart

Each lamp is built so it can stop being a lamp just as easily as it started being one. When the need passes, the parts go back to being a bucket, a rock, a bamboo pole — or head out to the garden or the compost pile — instead of staying stuck together as something only useful once. It’s a direct extension of the same question The Sustainabilitist Principles asked years earlier: what if a design didn’t exist?

Six lamps and counting

There have been six of these so far — built at school, at home, for colleagues along the way. It’s an ongoing, informal set of outcomes rather than a single finished object; the next one starts whenever the next need does.
