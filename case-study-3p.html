3P: People Processing Plastic

An Experiment in Plastic Reuse and Recycling: Passionate people performing practical plastic processing, producing prized products, preventing pollution proliferation, and progressing a pristine planet.

3P is a collaboration between Kristian Bjørnard, Chris Attenborough, and the product design department at the Maryland Institute College of Art. Designer objects, produced with repurposed HDPE and whatever other found plastic could be procured.

The critique

Plastic is a remarkable material — it can do almost anything. It’s also technically highly recyclable. In practice, it’s treated so disposably that it’s basically just trash: seen as so low-value that no one’s put in the effort to make it worth collecting and reusing properly. Treated that way, it ends up out in nature, where it’s not just clutter but poison.

Starting from Precious Plastic

3P drew its approach from Precious Plastic, an open-source, commons-based project for designing and building plastic grinding and melting machinery — the tools to turn found plastic into new objects and materials.

Ad hoc machinery

The machines for remaking plastic were built the same way as everything else — from as much found material as possible. The oven started as a home oven, stripped of its case and rebuilt. The grinder had its teeth cut from new steel, but everything else was assembled from whatever was on hand. Molds and other tools got made as they were needed, often repurposed from kitchen equipment that could already handle the heat.

Performance as method

The project grew into something closer to performance than production. Grinding, melting, kneading, molding — done in public — mattered more than any single object coming out of it. It gave us a platform to talk with people about why plastic needs to come out of the waste stream, and why it needs to be made desirable and valuable again instead of disposable.

What it became

3P turned into a curricular foundation for a circular economy class at MICA that’s since run for multiple semesters, and the product design program has built its own plastic recycling machinery to keep exploring — and performing — these ideas.
